DATA VISUALISATION AND PRESENTATION

LEARNING OUTCOMES

1: Critically evaluate and apply data visualisation grammar and idioms to the whole of the visualisation process and the resulting presentations.
2: Critically evaluate the capabilities of different visualisation tools and programming languages (proprietary and/or open source) to support the discovery and display of critical and valuable answers in different types of datasets.
3: Systematically implement key analysis and visualisation techniques using realistic data sources from disparate disciplines.
4: Effectively use the most appropriate visualisation tools in order to effectively present and communicate complex information and facilitate informed decision making.

REFLECTIONS
Data Analysis and Data Visualisation Techniques
Over the course of this module, my confidence in data analysis and statistical knowledge has improved particularly in how I use data visualisation to understand and communicate data insights.
I am now more relaxed when selecting visualisation techniques based on the structure of the data and the analytical goal, whether for exploratory data analysis (EDA), comparison, trend identification, or model evaluation.
I have developed a stronger comprehension of how visual elements such as axes, colour and scale influence interpretation.
Using bar charts to display categorical distributions, line charts for time based trends, and scatter plots for relationships between variables greatly improves clarity and insight. My data visualization grammar has increased and ensures my visualisations support accurate and meaningful conclusions rather than just a visual appeal.
An early challenge I faced was in deciding which visualisation type best suited a given dataset.
Through feedback and practice, I overcame this by focusing more on the analytical question first and simplifying visuals where necessary.


Using Tableau for Data Visualization
My experience with Tableau was largely helpful. Tableau’s drag and drop interface made it easy to quickly explore data and test different visual approaches without extensive coding.
Using filters with dashboards allowed me to explore data dynamically and helped me better understand user centered visualization principles.
My awareness of layout, visual hierarchy and consistency when presenting information also increased.
However, one challenge I encountered was overloading dashboards with too many charts or controls.
This taught me that effective dashboards focus on clarity and relevance over the quantity of information displayed.
Additionally, I noticed that while Tableau is excellent for visual analytics and presentation, it is less flexible for complex data preprocessing or advanced statistical modelling compared to programming languages like R or Python.


Producing Results Using R
Using R significantly enhanced my analytical capability and understanding of the data analysis process.
R was particularly effective for exploratory data analysis and statistical visualizations, especially using libraries such as ggplot2 and dplyr.
Writing R scripts helped me think more systematically about data cleaning, transformation, and reproducibility and with regular practice my confidence and efficiency improved.
Overall, using R reinforced the importance of transparent analysis and allowed more control over data processing and visualisation design.
Overall, I feel confident using a data visualisation techniques and tools to analyse and communicate data effectively.
Each tool, R, Python, and Tableau, has its own strengths, and this module helped me understand when and why to use each one.


Peer Feedback
Peer feedback provided by Francesca and Mattia was valuable in reinforcing the strengths of my comparative analysis while highlighting areas for further development.
Both confirmed that the report was clearly structured and grounded in visualisation theory, in relation to task abstraction, attributes, and the use of marks and channels.
This feedback reassured me that my application of frameworks such as Hibino’s task abstraction and established visual encoding principles was appropriate.
Francesca’s comments in encouraging deeper consideration of accessibility were especially helpful. While accessibility was acknowledged in the original analysis, her suggestion to examine specific issues such as screen reader compatibility, keyboard navigation, and WCAG contrast ratios highlighted an opportunity to strengthen my evaluation.
Mattia’s feedback reinforced my distinction between Ryanair’s attribute driven design and easyJet’s spatial, map based approach. His emphasis on spatial position as one of the most powerful visual encoding channels strengthened my understanding of why easyJet’s route map is effective for exploratory tasks.
His hybrid solution suggestion of combining spatial and price based information also encouraged me to think more critically about design improvements rather than limiting the analysis to comparison.
Overall, peer feedback enhanced my understanding of how different visualisation strategies support different cognitive tasks and user goals and helped me reflect on how future analyses could integrate accessibility considerations and design recommendations.


Tutor Feedback– Programming Exercise
The overall result of 65 reflects a good level of performance, particularly in terms of analytical competence and conceptual understanding, while highlighting areas where my work lacked critical depth and coherent structure.
One key area identified was the need to critically evaluate visualisation choice as I had not sufficiently justified their effectiveness or acknowledged their limitations.
The feedback has been valuable in helping me identify how to move from demonstrating knowledge to presenting a more critical and well integrated analysis.


Tutor Feedback – Dashboard Design
The tutor’s feedback confirmed that my dashboard proposal addressed the core objectives by clearly identifying the target audience and aligning the dashboard’s purpose with the historical nature of the World Cup dataset.
The recognition of analysts, commentators, and performance teams reinforced that my design decisions were grounded in real user needs, a requirement for fast, high-level insights and comparative trend analysis.
This dashboard design exercise improved my understanding of how effective data visualisation goes beyond chart selection and requires critical justification, clear metric definition, and strong user needs alignment.
While my initial focus was identifying an appropriate audience and selecting a suitable dashboard type, tutor feedback highlighted the importance of explaining why specific design decisions were made, rather than stating what was included.
Tutor feedback also made me reflect on user interaction and workflow design importance as I focused on filters and interactivity, I undervalued explaining how users actually engage with these features to explore comparisons and scenarios.
Developing this aspect strengthened my understanding of dashboards as interactive analytical tools rather than static visual reports.
