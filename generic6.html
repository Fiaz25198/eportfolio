Ethical, Legal, Social, and Professional Implications of Accessibility Failure
The AllTogether case study highlights the consequences of failing to effectively apply professional ethical codes in software development that can lead to significant legal, social, and professional consequences. Despite having an established accessibility policy aligned with the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) together with a Diversity Advisory Board, the company released a feature with known accessibility defects which demonstrated ethical lapses with legal, social, and professional implications.
ACM Principle 1.1: Contribute to society and human well being.
The company leadership’s dismissive questioning, “How many blind people use our product, anyway?” reflects an exclusionary approach that conflicts with the principle that all people are stakeholders in computing (Association for Computing Machinery, 2018). AllTogether limited access directly by reducing disabled users’ ability to perform their jobs. From a social perspective this reinforces digital exclusion and undermines equality, rather than promoting inclusive participation.
ACM Principle 1.2: Avoid harm Avoid harm was also violated (Association for Computing Machinery, 2018). Accessibility defects preventing keyboard and assistive technology use were identified during QA testing making it predictable and preventable. Nonetheless, the feature was released, resulting in users being unable to complete editing tasks independently. This created financial harm to both the company and its customers through increased support costs and software rollbacks and the social impact included frustration, loss of trust, and in some cases exclusion from essential work tasks Ethically, releasing known defects contradicts professional responsibility to minimise negative consequences. From a legal perspective, the release increased exposure to disability discrimination. claims across multiple jurisdictions. which requires reasonable accessibility in digital workplaces. WCAG is widely recognised as the accepted technical standard for demonstrating accessibility compliance (W3C, 2025).
ACM Principle 1.3: Be honest and trustworthy The company’s partial compliance is notable. Updating the Accessibility Conformance Report and creating a dedicated accessibility support channel demonstrated transparency. However, these actions occurred after release and did not prevent harm.
ACM Principle 1.4: Be fair and take action not to discriminate Highlights the responsibility of all computing professionals involved (Association for Computing Machinery, 2018). Designers, developers, product owners, and leadership enabled discrimination by allowing inaccessible design patterns to reach production. Releasing software that disadvantages disabled users constitutes indirect discrimination.
ACM Principle 2.3: Know and respect existing rules Explicit violation and clear breach. Ignoring these rules undermined governance and professionalism, resulting in wasted development effort and reputational risk. Respect for standards is a core element of professional competence.
BCS Code of Conduct The Public Interest principle requires computing professionals to safeguard wellbeing and avoid harm, which was not upheld (BCS, 2022). The duty of professional competence and integrity requires adherence to recognised standards such as WCAG, which was intentionally ignored. The duty to relevant authority was breached by disregarding internal policies, while the duty to the profession was undermined by normalising inaccessible design practices when it is treated as optional rather than fundamental.
In conclusion, the AllTogether case demonstrates that ethical codes are ineffective if they are not applied consistently in decision making. Failure to uphold ACM and BCS principles resulted in social exclusion, legal risk, increased costs, and reduced professional integrity. Embedding accessibility from the outset would have prevented harm, reduced risk, and delivered a higher quality product.

[Summary Post]
The AllTogether case study determined that accessibility failures are not purely technical defects but reflect significant ethical, legal, social, and organisational shortcomings. My initial analysis identified clear violations of ACM and BCS principles, in relation to contributing to human well-being, avoiding harm, fairness, and professional competence. The company’s decision to release software with known accessibility issues resulted in user exclusion, reduced trust, and reputational consequences, showing how ethical codes lose effectiveness when not actively applied in decision-making. Feedback from my peers provided important additions to my analysis. Ruben emphasised that the legal implications are more substantial and enforceable than initially discussed. Regulations such as the European Accessibility Act, the UK Equality Act 2010, and US laws (ADA and Section 508) highlight that accessibility is not optional but a legal requirement with significant risks. He also reframed it as a governance failure, where limited authority of the Diversity Advisory Board allowed known defects to reach production. This reinforces the idea that ethical frameworks must be supported by organisational structures and accountability mechanisms to influence outcomes effectively. Abdullah expanded the discussion by framing accessibility as a social justice issue, emphasising its connection to indirect discrimination and digital inequality. Inaccessible systems limit participation, independence, and employment opportunities for people with disabilities. His focus on inclusive design, continuous testing, and involving users with disabilities highlights practical ways to implant ethical responsibility into development processes, aligning with user-centered design principles. Linking these insights to the module learning outcomes, Unit 1 demonstrated the importance of ethical reasoning in computing where a deductive approach and inductive reasoning indicated that the release should not have proceeded, yet it did. Unit 2 showed that accessibility due to its relevance and real-world impact allowed for meaningful research questions on governance and compliance. Unit 3 highlighted research methods, such as qualitative approaches (e.g., interviews) and quantitative measures (e.g., compliance metrics) to capture both technical and human factors. In conclusion, the case exemplifies that accessibility must be treated as a core quality and ethical requirement, supported by legal awareness and strong governance.

Summary: This comparative analysis of the Ryanair and EasyJet websites highlights how each airline design strategies to support different user objectives and focus on visualization approaches, supported tasks, usability, and overall design effectiveness.
The analysis highlights how different visual encodings and interaction models shape user engagement and decision‑making in travel planning contexts (Munzner, 2014; Ware, 2013). easyJet relies on a geographical, map‑based interface, representing airports as points, regions and routes as spatial connections enabling users to visually explore destinations, discover unknown routes, and understand the airline’s network structure. Ryanair, however, bases its design on attribute‑driven information, using text, numbers, color‑coded pricing, and filtering tools such as its Fare Finder tool, allowing users to compare fares, sort by travel dates, and efficiently plan itineraries.
Peer‑responses reinforced and expanded on this analysis. Francesca emphasizes strong theoretical grounding to task abstraction frameworks (Hibino 1999) and accessibility considerations, noting how Ryanair reduces cognitive load by surfacing prices early, whereas easyJet’s design demands more navigation. Her recommendations for deeper exploration of accessibility issues such as screen‑reader behavior and contrast ratios, referenced WCAG standards. Mattia’s review highlighted effective differentiation of attributes, with Ryanair focusing on price and schedule variables and easyJet on spatial connectivity. He praised the clear explanation of marks and channels, while drawing attention to visualization principles to show that spatial position is a powerful encoding channel.
The design leverages spatial position as a primary visual encoding channel, which is widely recognized as one of the most effective means of representing relational and network data (Bertin, 1983; Ware, 2013).
Overall, the comparison illustrates how visual design choices, whether map‑based exploration or price‑centric filtering, outline user engagement and decision‑making. easyJet excels in exploratory, map‑based route discovery, while Ryanair is superior for price‑oriented, data‑driven trip planning. Both have matching strengths, and a hybrid approach that layers pricing onto route maps would combine the strengths of both platforms to better support comprehensive travel planning.
