Collaborative Discussion: Report on Ryanair and Easyjet Websites (Peer Response & Summary
Summary: This comparative analysis of the Ryanair and EasyJet websites highlights how each airline design strategies to support different user objectives and focus on visualization approaches, supported tasks, usability, and overall design effectiveness.
The analysis highlights how different visual encodings and interaction models shape user engagement and decision‑making in travel planning contexts (Munzner, 2014; Ware, 2013).
easyJet relies on a geographical, map‑based interface, representing airports as points, regions and routes as spatial connections enabling users to visually explore destinations, discover unknown routes, and understand the airline’s network structure.
Ryanair, however, bases its design on attribute‑driven information, using text, numbers, color‑coded pricing, and filtering tools such as its Fare Finder tool, allowing users to compare fares, sort by travel dates, and efficiently plan itineraries.
Peer‑responses reinforced and expanded on this analysis.
Francesca emphasizes strong theoretical grounding to task abstraction frameworks (Hibino 1999) and accessibility considerations, noting how Ryanair reduces cognitive load by surfacing prices early, whereas easyJet’s design demands more navigation.
Her recommendations for deeper exploration of accessibility issues such as screen‑reader behavior and contrast ratios, referenced WCAG standards.
Mattia’s review highlighted effective differentiation of attributes, with Ryanair focusing on price and schedule variables and easyJet on spatial connectivity.
He praised the clear explanation of marks and channels, while drawing attention to visualization principles to show that spatial position is a powerful encoding channel.
The design leverages spatial position as a primary visual encoding channel, which is widely recognized as one of the most effective means of representing relational and network data (Bertin, 1983; Ware, 2013).
Overall, the comparison illustrates how visual design choices, whether map‑based exploration or price‑centric filtering, outline user engagement and decision‑making.
easyJet excels in exploratory, map‑based route discovery, while Ryanair is superior for price‑oriented, data‑driven trip planning.
Both have matching strengths, and a hybrid approach that layers pricing onto route maps would combine the strengths of both platforms to better support comprehensive travel planning.
